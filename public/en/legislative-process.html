How a Bill Becomes Law

A general introduction to the stages a bill passes through to become law under Nepal's Constitution and the Federal Parliament's rules of procedure. This is not a live tracker for any specific bill's current status — see the official source linked below for that.

This page describes the general process only, not a live/current bill tracker. This site doesn't publish the current status of any specific bill, because that data isn't something we can keep reliably accurate and up to date (in line with our “never fabricate data” principle). To check a specific bill's current status, use the Federal Parliament Secretariat's own official “Bill” pages linked below.

1

1. Registration

A bill can be registered by the government (a government bill) or by an individual member (a private member's bill) in either the House of Representatives or the National Assembly, except for certain finance and security-related bills, which must be registered in the House of Representatives.

2

2. First reading & general discussion

The bill is introduced to the house, and members hold a general discussion on its principles and necessity.

3

3. Committee stage (clause-by-clause)

The bill goes to the relevant thematic committee, where it's discussed clause by clause, amendments are proposed, and expert or stakeholder input may be taken.

4

4. Amendment & passage in that house

The committee's report returns to the full house, amendments are debated and voted on, and the bill is passed or rejected by majority vote in that house.

5

5. The second house (bicameral process)

A bill passed by one house moves to the other house, where the same process — reading, committee, amendment, vote — repeats. The National Assembly's role is limited on finance bills.

6

6. Presidential authentication

Once passed by both houses, the bill is sent to the President for authentication. Once authenticated, it takes effect as an Act.
